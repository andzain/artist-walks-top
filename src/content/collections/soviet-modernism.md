## Curator's Note

For me, Soviet modernism is not simply an architectural style. It is a record of optimism, ambition, and contradiction. Every building tells a story about how a society imagined its future and how those ambitions changed over time.

Walking through Yerevan today means moving through layers of those ideas. Public buildings, housing complexes, monuments, and cultural institutions continue to shape everyday life, even when they have become almost invisible through familiarity.

## Why It Matters

Modernist architecture is often judged only by its appearance. Yet these buildings were designed as complete social projects, connecting housing, education, culture, transport, and public space into one vision of urban life.

Looking carefully reveals not only concrete forms, but also political ideals, artistic experimentation, and technological innovation.

## Looking Closer

Many of the most interesting details remain unnoticed: reliefs, mosaics, typography, sculptural entrances, landscaping, and the relationship between architecture and surrounding public space.

These small observations often reveal more than the buildings themselves.

## Experience this Collection

The Soviet Modernism Walk explores these places through the perspective of an artist and researcher, connecting architecture with history, public life, and contemporary Yerevan.

Every city preserves traces of the futures it once imagined. In Yerevan, modernist architecture remains one of the most visible records of those ambitions. Public buildings, cultural institutions, housing districts and monuments continue to shape everyday life, even when they become almost invisible through familiarity.

Rather than presenting modernism as a fixed historical style, this collection explores it as a living landscape. These buildings were designed not only as architectural objects but as parts of a broader vision for society—one where culture, education and public life formed a coherent urban experience.

Walking through Yerevan today reveals layers of optimism, contradiction and adaptation. Some buildings have been carefully preserved, others radically altered, and many quietly continue to serve the city decades after their construction.

Looking closely means noticing more than concrete forms. Reliefs, mosaics, typography, landscaping and the relationship between buildings and public space reveal stories that are often overlooked.

This collection introduces the architectural language that continues to define much of modern Yerevan and serves as the foundation for the Soviet Modernism walking experience.
