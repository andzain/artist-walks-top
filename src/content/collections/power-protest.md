Cities express political ideas through architecture long before they appear in history books. Government buildings, monuments, public squares and ceremonial avenues all reflect changing visions of power and collective identity.

Yerevan offers a remarkable opportunity to observe these transformations within a relatively compact urban landscape. Layers of imperial rule, Soviet planning, independence and civic movements coexist within walking distance of one another.

Public space is never neutral. A square designed for military parades may later become the stage for democratic protest. Monuments created to celebrate authority can acquire entirely new meanings as society changes.

This collection invites visitors to look beyond individual buildings and consider the relationships between architecture, memory and political life. Every monument, boulevard and government complex becomes part of a wider conversation about how cities express authority—and how citizens reclaim those same spaces.

The accompanying walking experience follows these places on foot, revealing the stories that connect architecture with political history and everyday urban life.
